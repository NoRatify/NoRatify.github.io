WHAT SUCKS ABOUT THE CURRENT TA?

Wages

This is the big one. We’ve been told that “most Postdocs will receive a 20-23% salary increase” by October 2023. This is misleading. Here’s why:

  • The “20-23% salary increase” estimate combines step increases, based on experience, with overall scale increases, meant to offset inflation and cost of living
  • The initial scale increases–which represent the true “growth” in our pay scale–will be instituted in two phases: ~8% in April and ~8% in October, for a total of ~16% by October 2023. Subsequent scale increases will be only 3.5% per year.
  • ○
    This will apply only to Postdocs who are on scale (Postdocs currently paid above scale will not see these increases)
  • ○
    Postdocs paid above-scale (~40% of Postdocs are currently above-scale) will see only 3% yearly increases
  • The base pay in the TA still falls short of 1st year salaries at competing institutions:
  • ○
    St Jude - $70k
  • ○
    Stanford - $68k
  • ○
    Harvard Med - $65k
  • ○
    MIT - $65K

When we look at the scale increases, accounting for inflation, the current proposed pay scale constitutes a pay cut for Postdocs:

  • Inflation is bad now, and has been for some time. Inflation over the past year, while this contract was negotiated, was ~7.7%. Inflation the previous year was ~5.4%.
  • ○
    The Social Security Administration issued a 5.9% COLA increase for 2021 and an 8.7% COLA increase for 2022
  • If inflation in 2023 remains steady, the proposed initial 16% increase from our current pay scale over the year-long phase-in period constitutes at best, wage stagnation, and at worst, a pay cut
  • Following this initial phase-in period, scale increases will be 3.5% per year, far below the current rate of inflation, or even the average rate over the past couple of years
  • ○
    Even lumping together experienced-based step increases with scale increases, the total yearly pay increase an on-scale Postdoc will see is 7.2% - STILL BELOW the current rate of inflation
  • ○
    And even if combining step and scale increases keeps up with inflation in future years, this essentially means that Postdocs don’t deserve step increases (i.e. raises) based on experience
  • ○
    Postdocs paid above-scale (~40% of Postdocs) will only see 3% yearly increases 
  • Locks us into this pay scale for 4.5 years with no way to negotiate COLA or improved raises should our pay continue to lag behind inflation and cost of living in CA

This pay scale not only fails to make up for the effective wage decreases we’ve seen over the past several years due to our previous contract not keeping up with inflation, it continues to exacerbate the problem with scale increases below the current rate of inflation.

The result is that the proposed salary scale constitutes a pay cut for all Postdocs that gets progressively worse over the course of the contract. 

In addition to the effective pay cut, there are several other reasons why the proposed pay scale is not a win: 

  • Through October of 2023, Postdocs at the bottom two steps of the pay scale will still be paid less than the minimum state salary for exempt workers, which will go to $64,480 as of Jan. 1, 2023
  • ○
    There is NO commitment from the UC to increase our salary should the state institute further minimum wage increases
  • The proposed pay scale reflects essentially no movement on the UC’s part from the scale proposed before the strike began
  • ○
    The only improvement from pre-strike wage proposals is an additional 2.5% scale increase in the first year and an additional 0.5% yearly scale increase after that for Postdocs on scale

The wages in the current TA do not reflect “wins” concordant with the historic strike efforts that we have undertaken. They are essentially a continuation of our current (bad) wages.

Childcare

The TA includes a childcare subsidy that starts at $2,500 per YEAR. In many cities where there are UC campuses, childcare for a single child can cost upwards of $2,000 per MONTH. This subsidy does little to reduce the burden on Postdoc parents.

A couple more egregious details:

  • This benefit is specifically a subsidy for licensed childcare providers, meaning that Postdoc parents who cannot afford the total cost of childcare from a licensed provider will receive no additional money above their regular salary to help pay for costs associated with raising a child. 
  • At many of our campuses, the UC-provided childcare is one of the only, if not the only option for childcare in the area, meaning that:
  • ○
    The UC sets the unreasonably high price of childcare,
  • ○
    Many parents who will collect the childcare subsidy will pay it back to the UC through their childcare services. 
  • ○
    The UC will not have to pay out the subsidy to any parents who obtain childcare through a family member, friend, neighbour, or other non-licensed source. 
  • This makes the subsidy essentially unusable for many Postdocs

International Workers

International postdocs represent the majority (~60%) of all postdocs working across UC campuses. 

A standalone “international workers” article was withdrawn from the TA. Aspects of the agreement that specifically affect international postdocs are implicitly and explicitly stated elsewhere in various articles of the agreement. Key elements are outlined below.

Positive/Neutral Outcomes:

  • Initial appointments will be for 2 years (up from 1), which provides greater protection against early and unjust dismissal within the first year, and allows greater flexibility to obtain either a J-1 or H-1B visa.
  • ○
    H-1B benefits include no two-year home country physical presence requirement, can be expedited for a fee, and allows the visa-holder to file an immigrant visa petition
  • ○
    H-1B drawbacks include more expensive fees, longer processing time, spouse and children dependents not allowed to work except for limited situations.
  • If an international postdoc is found to not be allowed to work in the US legally, or in cases where a postdoc’s immigration and/or work status in the US is affected by changes in legislation (e.g. repeal of DACA), the university and the UAW will meet and attempt to re-employ the postdoc
  • Guaranteed leave to renew J-1 Visas

Negative Outcomes:

  • Leave to renew J-1 Visas is unpaid
  • ○
    Entry visas, such as the J-1, allow for temporary entry into the United States, and the J-1 can only be renewed outside of the country.
  • ○
    To obtain a new J-1, postdocs have to leave the country (typically returning home), attend a consular interview, and then wait to receive their passport containing the new J-1 visa in the mail. Timelines are variable, and it’s difficult to predict and plan travel.
  • ○
    Although this is a necessary component of the international scholar experience, the necessary leave for visa renewal is unpaid.
  • Fees required to obtain the necessary documentation for entry and work in the US will not be reimbursed by the UC . These fees (e.g. SEVIS and visa fees) can amount to several hundreds to thousands of dollars.
  • Under the TA’d contract, postdocs (international and otherwise) will receive no monetary support to help with their move to work at the UC.
  • ○
    International postdocs typically face excessive travel expenses, lack of access to local currency (USD) upon arrival (and for one month after starting work), and difficulties obtaining a social security number, permanent residence, and even a bank account. 
  • ○
    A proposed article would have seen the UC provide a meager $5,000 reimbursement for relocation expenses. Even this was withdrawn.

Meeting Access Needs for Temporarily or Permanently Disabled Workers

Many of our colleagues with disabilities feel disregarded in this contract. Accessibility concerns were addressed in proposed “Access Needs” and “Public Health and Safety” articles. These were thrown out in bargaining.

Core demands from our colleagues with disabilities, now dismantled in the current TA, are:

  • Universal Online Access without Medical Documentation
  • ○
    Moves the UC beyond the bare minimum guaranteed by the ADA to create a truly inclusive/accessible workplace
  • ○
    Removes obstacles that prevent the disabled from conducting their work
  • ○
    Would adopt a “highest common denominator” accessibility perspective, guaranteeing access for ALL disabled UC workers and affording safety for high-risk individuals 
  • ○
    Removes the onerous requirement for medical documentation and review before workers are granted disability-related access needs.
  • Variable access to clinician appointments, referrals, testing, and diagnosis can make it impossible to obtain the necessary paperwork with a reasonable timeframe, cost, etc., presenting an additional barrier to disabled workers
  • ●
    Lifting of ban on instructor-requested masking and other layers of COVID protection

Housing

The originally proposed housing article, which was completely dropped, would have guaranteed university subsidized housing, and if not available, the UC would ensure rent would not exceed 30% of your salary. This was our attempt to control for local costs of living and keep up with spiraling rents in California and has no equivalent in the current TA.

Implications for Graduate Students

While we don’t know where the graduate student unions will land, we can be fairly certain that whatever contract we accept will be used as a wage cap and comparison. This would make it extremely difficult for the grad students to get anywhere near their current demands.

Additionally, the TA  includes a “No Strike” clause that would go into effect immediately upon ratification. While “No Strike” clauses are standard in union contracts, the timing of our planned ratification vote means that ours could go into effect while Grad students are still bargaining, meaning that we could not continue our strike in solidarity. This undermines the original strategy of our strike, which was that all 4 units gain power from striking together. The duration of each unit’s contract is such that it will be very unlikely for us all to be out of contract again at the same time, making this a unique opportunity to bargain while supported by the power of all 4 units striking.

What can I do about it?

While the 5810 bargaining team has been working hard to negotiate a contract with the UC, it is ultimately up to us, the members of the union, to decide whether or not the TA represents a contract that works for us. 

If it doesn't, we have the right to vote NO and refuse to ratify the current TA.

Here is a handy flowchart of questions to consider as you decide how to vote:

What happens if I vote NO?

Refusing to ratify the TA would send a strong message to the UC that this offer isn’t good enough. 

If greater than 50% of voting Postdocs vote NO on this TA, the UAW and the UC will have to return to the bargaining table. A NO vote does lead to a less certain future than a YES vote, but it also opens up a number of paths that could lead us to a more fair and equitable contract.

What Happens After a NO vote?
After a NO vote, there are a few possible eventualities we can plan for. Which path we end up on depends on a number of factors. Here are the possibilities:

  • The current UAW Bargaining Team (BT) and the UC could negotiate an improved TA, which would then be returned to us for a vote
  • ○
    Can we get a worse proposal than we did before the no vote?
  • This is very unlikely. For one thing, UC would know that this offer wouldn’t be good enough to ratify. For another, the UC walking back offers already on the table could strengthen our ULPs (Unfair Labor Practices), and UC has a bad track record with committing ULPs during bargaining. Most PERB (board that administers labor relations laws in CA) decisions have gone against UC and in favor of unions. 
  • ○
    It is also possible that some or all of the current BT would resign, and a new BT would be elected to continue negotiations
  • The UC could attempt to declare an impasse
  • ○
    It is not clear that UC can declare an impasse unilaterally. In order to declare an impasse, PERB has to agree thatboth parties have been bargaining in good faith and that no further movement can be made at the bargaining table. 
  • The outstanding Requests for Information (RFI) and Unfair Labor Practice (ULP) complaints the UAW has against the UC would likely block the UC from being able to take this path.
  • The employer usually submits a proposal that they call their “last, best, final offer” to signal that they will not offer anything past this offer. If PERB decides there’s no impasse, the two parties go back to bargaining with each other
  • PERB could declare an impasse
  • ○
    PERB may determine that an impasse exists when “the parties have reached a point in meeting and conferring at which their differences in positions are such that further meetings would be futile." (Higher Education Employer-Employee Relations Act [HEERA], Government Code Section 3562(j).) 
  • ○
    In this case, a third-party mediator would be brought in by the state
  • ○
    This mediator would facilitate bargaining and suggest compromise. Mediation is non-binding, and largely works with the mediator “going between” the UC and UAW negotiating teams, much like bargaining.
  • May call a fact-finding hearing
  • Supposed to pick a reasonable proposal that works for both parties
  • We are optimistic that this would lead to a better contract given:
  • ○
    The dire financial situation(like with our current rent burden and housing crisis)
  • ○
    That the UC has barely budged on financial proposals during negotiation
  • ○
    Outstanding ULPs against the UC

Would we still be on strike?
There are three ways to end a union-sanctioned strike: 

  1. Ratify a contract
  2. Rank and file votes to end strike
  3. A mandate is issued by the international executive board of UAW to end the strike

This means that it is possible we would continue the strike, or that we would pause the strike to rework bylaws and/or elect a new bargaining team. Exactly how this proceeds depends partly on what next steps the UAW chooses to take, and what we, as rank and file members, decide. If did vote to end the strike, as long as we are not under contract, we could later vote to go on strike again to maximize power at the bargaining table.

The most important thing bargaining after a NO vote is that we unite again as one union to try to get the best contract possible for everyone. We all have the same goal! Let’s get as much as possible from UC.

What are the Upsides of Voting No?

  • Sends a clear message that the proposed Tentative Agreement is not acceptable, and that the UC has to do better
  • Gives the Bargaining Team additional leverage to push back on UC: it shows that the members they represent will not accept that particular contract
  • Capitalizes on the momentum of the moment and the scale of the 4-unit strike to push for real gains
  • Provides an opportunity to change the bargaining process to one that is more democratic and inclusive
  • ○
    We have already started holding caucus meetings, conducting polls, and cross-campus organizing as rank and file so could continue this open communication
  • ○
  • After a no vote rank and file will need to get more involved in bargaining decisions so that we can find a contract that will get a yes vote. We can revise by-laws, continue getting more educated about bargaining, and vote on bargaining decisions
  • ○
    Changes should be voted on by as many people as possible, as much as possible
  • Likely to eventually result in a more favorable contract

What are the Downsides?

  • Could extend the length of strike
  • Delays beginning of new contract
  • Opens up new routes for how the bargaining will proceed, which could potentially be ​lengthy, less straightforward, or convoluted]
  • Once a TA has been rejected, articles that have been TA’ed can be reopened and bargained over again WITHOUT it being considered regressive bargaining. However, it is highly unlikely that we end up in a worse place (see above points on “What Happens After A NO Vote?”)
