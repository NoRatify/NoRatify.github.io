What?! But this is a historic, monumental achievement!

Indeed, academic workers have been under UC's foot for so long that even small concessions seem like a win.

What has the bargaining team accomplished?

  • Increased paid parental leave (from 4 weeks to 8 weeks) for all Postdocs
  • Marginally improved security for international postdocs:
  • ○
    Leave to renew visas
  • ○
    Two-year initial appointments
  • Some improvements around grievance arbitration and anti-bullying/abusive conduct measures

What sucks about the current Tentative Agreement (TA)?

Wages

This is the big one. We’ve been told that “most Postdocs will receive a 20-23% salary increase” by October 2023. This is misleading. Here’s why:

  • The “20-23% salary increase” estimate combines step increases, based on experience, with overall scale increases, meant to offset inflation and cost of living
  • The initial scale increases–which represent the true “growth” in our pay scale–will be instituted in two phases: ~8% in April and ~8% in October, for a total of ~16% by October 2023. Subsequent scale increases will be only 3.7% per year.

When we look at the scale increases, accounting for inflation, the current proposed pay scale constitutes a pay cut for Postdocs:

  • Inflation is bad now, and has been for some time. Inflation over the past year, while this contract was negotiated, was ~7.7%. Inflation the previous year was ~5.4%.
  • ○
    The Social Security Administration issued a 5.9% COLA increase for 2021 and an 8.7% COLA increase for 2022
  • If inflation in 2023 remains steady, the proposed initial 16% increase from our current pay scale over the year-long phase-in period constitutes at best, wage stagnation, and at worst, a pay cut
  • Following this initial phase-in period, scale increases will be 3.5% per year, far below the current rate of inflation
  • ○
    Even lumping together experienced-based step increases with scale increases, the total yearly pay increase an on-scale Postdoc will see is 7.2% - STILL BELOW the current rate of inflation
  • ○
    And even if combining step and scale increases keeps up with inflation in future years, this essentially means that Postdocs don’t deserve step increases (i.e. raises) based on experience
  • ○
    Postdocs paid above-scale (~40% of Postdocs) will only see 3% yearly increases 
  • Locks us into this pay scale for 4.5 years with no way to negotiate COLA or improved raises should our pay continue to lag behind inflation and cost of living in CA

This pay scale not only fails to make up for the effective wage decreases we’ve seen over the past several years due to our previous contract not keeping up with inflation, it continues to exacerbate the problem with scale increases below the current rate of inflation.

The result is that the proposed scale constitutes a pay cut for all Postdocs that gets progressively worse over the course of the contract. 

In addition to the effective pay cut, there are several other reasons why the proposed pay scale is not a win: 

  • Through October of 2023, Postdocs at the bottom two steps of the pay scale will still be paid less than the minimum state salary for exempt workers, which will go to $64,480 as of Jan. 1, 2023
  • ○
    There is NO commitment from the UC to increase our salary should the state institute further minimum wage increases
  • The proposed pay scale reflects essentially no movement on the UC’s part from the scale proposed before the strike began
  • ○
    The only improvement from pre-strike wage proposals is an additional .5% yearly scale increase

The wages in the current TA do not reflect “wins” concordant with the historic strike efforts that we have undertaken. They are essentially a continuation of our current (bad) wages.

Childcare

The TA includes a childcare subsidy that starts at $2,500 per YEAR. In many cities where there are UC campuses, childcare for a single child can cost upwards of $2,000 per MONTH. This subsidy does little to reduce the burden on Postdoc parents.

A couple more egregious details:

  • This benefit is specifically a subsidy for licensed childcare providers, meaning that Postdoc parents who cannot afford the total cost of childcare from a licensed provider will receive no additional money above their regular salary to help pay for costs associated with raising a child. 
  • At many of our campuses, the UC-provided childcare is one of the only, if not the only option for childcare in the area, meaning that:
  • ○
    The UC sets the unreasonably high price of childcare,
  • ○
    Many parents who will collect the childcare subsidy will pay it back to the UC through their childcare services. 
  • ○
    The UC will not have to pay out the subsidy to any parents who obtain childcare through a family member, friend, neighbour, or other non-licensed source. 

International Workers

Leave for visa renewal is unpaid.

Meeting Access Needs for Temporarily or Permanently Disabled Workers

Many of our colleagues with disabilities feel disregarded in this contract. The main accessibility concerns, addressed in proposed (but discarded in bargaining) “Access Needs” and “Public Health and Safety” articles, are:

  • Universal Online Access
  • ○
    The UC has been essentially ignoring the Americans with Disabilities Act for 32 years. Universal access removes obstacles that prevent the disabled from conducting their work
  • ○
    In lieu of universal masking, PCR testing, contact tracing, ventilation upgrades, and other important protective measures the UC does not currently provide against COVID-19, universal online access affords safety for high-risk individuals.
  • Remove the onerous requirement for medical documentation and review before workers are granted disability-related access needs.
  • ○
    This is important, because variable access to clinician appointments, referrals, testing, and diagnosis can make it impossible to obtain the necessary paperwork with a reasonable timeframe, cost, etc
  • ○
    This puts undue pressure on, and requires superfluous labor from workers with disabilities.

Implications for Graduate Students

While we don’t know where the graduate student unions will land, we can be fairly certain that whatever contract we accept will be used as a wage cap and comparison. This would make it extremely difficult for the grad students to get anywhere near their current demands.

Additionally, the TA  includes a “No Strike” clause that would go into effect immediately upon ratification. While Not Strike clauses are standard in union contracts, the timing of our planned ratification vote means that ours could go into effect while Grad students are still bargaining, meaning that we could not continue our strike in solidarity.

What can I do about it?

While the bargaining team has been working hard to negotiate a contract with the UC, it is ultimately up to us, the members of the union, to decide whether or not the TA represents a contract that works for us. 

If it doesn't, we have the right to vote NO and refuse to ratify the current TA.

Here is a handy flowchart of questions to consider as you decide how to vote:

What happens if I vote NO?

Refusing to ratify the TA would send a strong message to the UC that this offer isn’t good enough. 

If greater than 50% of voting Postdocs vote NO on this TA, the UAW and the UC will have to return to the bargaining table with the 2018 contract as the baseline. In the meantime, the strike would continue.

From there, a few different things could happen:

  • The current UAW Bargaining Team (BT) and the UC could negotiate an improved TA, which would then be returned to us for a vote
  • ○
    It is also possible that some or all of the current BT would resign, and a new BT would be elected to continue negotiations
  • The UC could attempt to declare an impasse
  • ○
    It is unclear whether this would be possible. In order to declare an impasse, PERB has to agree that both parties have been bargaining in good faith. The outstanding Requests for Information (RFI) and Unfair Labor Practice (ULP) complaints the UAW has against the UC could block the UC from being able to take this path
  • Should an impasse be declared, there are two possible routes:
  • ○
    The UC unilaterally applies the contract. If they do this, they cannot go any lower than their final offer (meaning the current TA)
  • ○
    A third party could be brought in to arbitrate the contract. The outstanding ULPs and RFIs could give the UAW additional leverage in this situation.

Here is Columbia University’s FAQ on NO votes from when they refused to ratify a TA

What are the upsides?

  • Sends a clear message that the proposed Tentative Agreement is not acceptable, and that the UC has to do better
  • Gives the Bargaining Team additional leverage to push back on UC
  • Capitalizes on the momentum of the moment/scale of the strike to push for real gains
  • Likely to eventually result in a more favorable contract

What are the downsides?

  • Could extend the length of strike
  • Delays beginning of new contract
  • Opens up new routes for how the bargaining will proceed, which could potentially be lengthy or convoluted
